# IT Governance Assessment – Gynae360

## Background  
Gynae360 is a private healthcare provider based in Kingston, Jamaica, offering gynecology, obstetrics, and fertility services. The organization operates under a centralized leadership model, with executive oversight concentrated at the CEO level.

IT capabilities are primarily delivered through an external service provider, which functions as the organization’s de facto IT department. While this model supports day-to-day operations, it limits the development of internal governance structures and reduces visibility into IT decision-making, performance, and risk.

The objective of this assessment was to evaluate the current IT governance environment, identify structural and operational gaps, and determine the organization’s readiness to support growth, regulatory compliance, and increased reliance on digital systems.

## Current Governance Structure  
Gynae360 does not operate with a formal IT governance framework. There are no defined governance bodies, committees, or structured oversight mechanisms for IT.

Responsibility for IT is largely distributed between executive leadership and the external IT provider. The CEO retains final authority over key decisions, particularly those affecting operations or requiring financial approval. Technical decisions are generally delegated to the external provider, based on their expertise and recommendations.

There is no formal documentation of governance roles, responsibilities, or decision rights. As a result, governance activities are executed informally, with limited standardization or consistency across the organization.

## IT Decision-Making  
IT decision-making is centralized and reactive in nature. Strategic and high-impact decisions are escalated to the CEO, particularly when they affect operational continuity or exceed typical expenditure thresholds.

Technical decisions are influenced by the external IT provider, who evaluates system requirements and recommends solutions. In some cases, decision authority varies depending on the system in question—for example, administrative software may fall under technical oversight, while core medical systems are directly influenced by executive leadership.

There is no formally defined decision-making framework, escalation model, or governance workflow. Decisions are made based on immediate needs, vendor input, and informal consultation rather than structured evaluation criteria.

## IT Budget & Investment  
The organization does not maintain a formal IT budgeting process. IT expenditure is managed on an as-needed basis, typically initiated through vendor quotations when a requirement arises.

There is no structured financial planning for IT, and no defined process for evaluating return on investment (ROI), total cost of ownership, or strategic value. Decisions are largely driven by operational necessity rather than long-term planning or prioritization.

This approach limits the organization’s ability to forecast IT needs, manage costs effectively, or align investment with broader business objectives.

## Governance Gaps  

### Absence of Formal Governance Structures  
There is no IT Steering Committee or equivalent governance body to provide oversight, define priorities, or ensure alignment with business strategy. Decision authority is concentrated and not formally distributed.

### Lack of Documented Policies and Procedures  
The organization does not maintain formal IT policies, standards, or procedures. This includes the absence of documented guidance for system usage, access control, data handling, and incident response.

### Limited Strategic Alignment  
IT is not integrated into strategic planning. There is no defined IT roadmap or structured approach to aligning technology initiatives with business goals.

### Informal Investment and Vendor Management  
IT investments are evaluated on an ad hoc basis without standardized criteria. Vendor risk assessment and procurement processes are not formalized.

### Lack of Architecture and Systems Governance  
There is no documented IT architecture. System integration decisions are based on compatibility and immediate need, rather than long-term design principles or scalability considerations.

### Absence of Performance Measurement  
The organization does not track IT performance through KPIs, service metrics, or operational indicators. There is no mechanism for evaluating the effectiveness or value of IT services.

### Weak Risk and Compliance Structures  
While there is awareness of regulatory obligations such as the Jamaica Data Protection Act, there are no formal governance mechanisms to ensure ongoing compliance, risk monitoring, or control enforcement.

## Governance Effectiveness  
Based on the assessment, the current governance environment reflects a low level of maturity. Governance processes are informal, undocumented, and highly dependent on individual decision-makers.

While the organization maintains basic operational functionality, the absence of structured governance introduces risks related to:

- inconsistent decision-making  
- limited accountability  
- reduced visibility into IT performance and risk  
- challenges in scaling operations  
- potential exposure to regulatory and data protection risks  

The reliance on an external provider without corresponding internal governance structures further limits control and oversight.

## Conclusion  
Gynae360’s current IT governance model is sufficient for basic operations but is not sustainable as the organization grows or increases its reliance on digital systems.

The absence of formal governance structures, policies, and performance monitoring limits the organization’s ability to manage risk, optimize investment, and align IT with business objectives.

A transition to a structured governance framework is required to support operational resilience, regulatory compliance, and long-term scalability. This includes establishing formal governance bodies, defining decision rights, implementing policies and controls, and introducing performance and risk management mechanisms.
